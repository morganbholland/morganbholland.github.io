Statement on Teaching

Introduction

As an instructor over the span of seven semesters, my teaching has focused on economic data analysis, including fundamental statistics and introductory econometrics. In addition, I have been a Teaching Assistant in Economics of Population and Financial Markets, Banking, and Monetary Policy. My experiences have led me to integrate three core values into my courses:

  1. Students have ownership over their own learning,
  2. Economic and statistical theories and models must be applied to real-world data,
  3. New knowledge is built on prior knowledge.

Student Ownership of Learning

I recognize that ultimately students are responsible for what they learn in pursuit of their own educations. My role is to be a facilitator, an advocate, and an evaluator, rather than a repository or regurgitator of information. To help students take ownership of their educations, I incorporate learning objectives, group discussions and work, and an attitude of humility about my own lifelong learning.

To incorporate learning objectives, I discuss what students will learn at the beginning of each new section of material. I also incorporate pretests at the beginning of each course, so students can compare their knowledge and skill at the end of a course to what they knew before the course started. Showing students that they are accomplishing learning objectives gives them confidence in their own ability to learn.

I also incorporate class discussions, small group activities, and larger group projects. Class discussions give students an opportunity to demonstrate their knowledge and understanding outside of official tests and quizzes. Small group activities and larger group projects are implemented so that students strengthen their knowledge and confidence by teaching their classmates new concepts and by hearing concepts from the perspective of their peers.

Finally, it is important to approach classes with humility and honesty to model lifelong learning. Acknowledging my own errors and holes in knowledge gives me an opportunity to continue my own education and to show students that learning does not stop when they have completed their coursework. Additionally, showing students my own learning process demonstrates how I have taken ownership of my continuing education and models how they can do the same.

Applying Theories to Data

I have found that traditional approaches to teaching economics and statistics leave students well-versed in economic and statistical theory, but unprepared to successfully apply these theories to problems and data. Part of my responsibilities as an instructor is to guide students in developing the critical thinking skills necessary to choose appropriate theories and models to answer research questions. To show that students must use discretion in applying economic theories, I show examples of how theories are supported by the data, as well as examples of how they are not. I also present debates in economics where it is unclear which economic theories match data. This allows students to develop their own critical thinking about economic theory and data. I teach students to see economic models through the lens of data and to use econometric models to validate and refine the economic theories taught in courses.

New Knowledge is Built on Prior Knowledge

It is important to relate the new knowledge students are learning to knowledge they already possess. If students can build associations between what they already know and what they are learning, it is easier for them to achieve mastery in the subject. I integrate this philosophy into my teaching through lectures and reviews. In lectures, I tie new knowledge to something students have seen in a previous course or to something I have taught in a previous class session. I also incorporate frequent reviews into my lectures so that students can better form the links between what they know and what they are learning.

This philosophy is especially important from a diversity perspective. I recognize that students come from many different backgrounds and have had many different experiences. I believe this is a strength students bring to my classroom because students are able to generate and share their own examples of how the knowledge they acquire in the course relates to their own lives. In addition, to make it easier for students to make connections to the skills and information they are learning, I make multiple connections to prior knowledge and adjust my expectations and examples based on the feedback of my students.

Conclusion

By integrating my values into teaching, I demonstrate to students that I am committed to their success. Another way I am committed to student success is through continuous refinement of my values and methods. The ideas I have presented here are not static. Instead, I am committed to continuously improving my teaching philosophies and methodologies through critical self-reflection and by integrating student and peer feedback into the classroom.
